# The Design Fix

**Try this thought experiment.** Name a policy that both a committed Trump voter and an Elizabeth Warren supporter want. Not a symbolic gesture — a real policy with a real majority behind it.

Hard, right? The way American politics is presented, these two constituencies want opposite things on every issue. It's not just a matter of emphasis or priorities — the entire frame of our political conversation insists that they are in fundamental opposition, and one must defeat the other.

Here's what happened when we ran the numbers.

We took survey responses from 45,707 Americans — the 2024 Cooperative Election Study, one of the largest academic surveys of American political opinion, weighted to match the national population — and asked what their government would look like if the electoral system let them express their actual preferences. Then we built that government and ran a legislative simulation.

The result: the same congress simultaneously extends the 2017 tax cuts and raises the corporate tax rate from 21% to 28%. The same congress passes DACA protections for Dreamers and increases border patrol funding and denies asylum at the border. The same congress passes an assault rifle ban and near-universal background check requirements while failing to expand concealed carry.

Not as a grand bargain. Not as a compromise where everyone gets half of what they want. As a legislature where different policy questions have different majority coalitions — because the American electorate, when it can actually express itself, is not divided into two homogeneous blocs on opposite sides of every issue.

That congress doesn't exist in America. But we know exactly what it would look like. And we know why it doesn't exist: design.

---

## It's Not Them. It's the Rules.

The current dysfunction in American politics — the paralysis, the mutual contempt, the sense that no one in Washington represents ordinary people — is not destiny. It is the output of three specific design choices, each made at a particular historical moment for reasons that no longer apply. None of them are natural laws. All of them can be changed.

**First-past-the-post voting** forces all political competition into two oversized tents held together not by shared agenda but by mutual fear of the other side. The Republican coalition contains a private equity executive and a laid-off factory worker who agree on almost nothing except not being Democrats. The Democratic coalition contains a Yale-educated civil rights attorney and a Pentecostal home health aide who share some policy goals but come from completely different political worlds. The party system manufactures these alliances by making the alternative — losing to the other side — worse than maintaining an incoherent coalition.

**Winner-take-all representation** means the 49% who voted for the losing candidate have zero representation. In a congressional district, school board race, or state legislature, the losing side's voters simply vanish from the map. This produces what millions of Americans experience as the feeling of illegitimacy — being governed by people you didn't choose, whose agenda has nothing to do with yours, on a recurring cycle. It's not paranoia. It's arithmetic.

**The primary system** means that public money funds a process where the most activated 15–20% of each party picks both candidates for everyone else. More than 40% of American voters are registered independents, unaffiliated with either major party. Most of them cannot participate in the primaries that determine who the actual choices will be in November. The process that effectively decides who governs is closed to the plurality of American voters. The people who do participate — smaller, more ideologically intense, more reliably mobilized — pick candidates who must then sprint toward the center for the general election while hoping voters don't notice the pivot.

Each of these is a design choice. Each was made by specific people at specific historical moments. None appear in the Constitution. All have real alternatives.

## What America Actually Looks Like

We ran 45,707 survey responses through a proportional representation simulation — the same type of multi-party system used in most of the developed world. Not to model a utopia. To ask a simple empirical question: if the American electoral system let people vote for a party that actually matched their views, what would the map look like?

Nine parties emerged. Not assigned. Discovered — extracted by clustering algorithm from how Americans actually answer 172 policy and demographic questions about their own lives and beliefs. Here is what the data found.

**The largest surprise is a party that barely exists in political conversation.**

Solidarity represents about 18% of the electorate — the third-largest party in the simulation. Majority female. Majority non-white. The lowest household income of any party in the system. 37.7% born-again evangelical. They believe elections are run unfairly at about the same rate as MAGA voters — 55.8% say national elections aren't fair. But their politics are center-left: they support labor protections, DACA, Medicaid expansion, and oppose increased police funding. They are not reliably Democratic, not reliably Republican, and largely absent from political conversation as a coherent constituency.

44% of them didn't vote in 2020. That isn't apathy. It is the rational response of a constituency that has no accurate political home.

The two-party system has no place for a voter who is majority non-white, low-income, female-majority, evangelical, economically left, and deeply distrustful of institutions. So rather than misrepresenting them, it largely ignores them — and they return the favor.

**The second surprise is a party everyone claims to want but nobody can find.**

Center represents about 12% of the electorate. Voted 41.9% for Trump in 2020. Also: the most trusting of elections of any party in the entire system — only 17.5% say national elections are run unfairly. They trusted the elections Biden won while voting for Trump. They support universal background checks, Medicaid expansion, same-sex marriage recognition, and border enforcement — all in the same person. They are the most genuinely cross-pressured constituency in American politics: neither party's reliable member, both parties' constant courtship target, nobody's actual representative.

**The third surprise is a party whose economic politics don't match its brand.**

Reform represents about 14% of the electorate. 61.6% say elections are unfair. 88.9% want increased border patrol. Strongly for the 2017 tax cuts. Strongly against DACA. So far, MAGA-adjacent. But also: 26.4% support raising the corporate tax rate. More than a quarter of the most immigration-restrictionist party outside the Nationalists supports higher corporate taxes — more support than most centrist Democrats have delivered in Republican-leaning districts. Reform is economically populist, not pro-business. The two-party system currently packages them with the pro-corporate wing of the Republican Party, which is why they feel perpetually betrayed by the people they elect.

These aren't theoretical constructs. They are Americans describing themselves, in their own words, on their own terms.

## The Fix Is Three Mechanisms

The same countries that use these mechanisms — most of Western Europe, New Zealand, Australia — produce coalition governments that pass legislation, represent diverse constituencies, and don't require you to vote against your values to avoid catastrophe.

**Proportional representation for the House.** The current system elects one representative per district who nominally represents everyone, which in practice means representing the 51% who voted for them and ignoring the rest. A five-member district under proportional voting works differently: each cluster of roughly 17% of the electorate earns a seat. A Democrat in a red district gets representation. A Republican in a blue city gets representation. Gerrymandering becomes mathematically incoherent — you cannot draw a line that silences a constituency when every fifth of the electorate earns a voice. A candidate stops being an employee of the national party and becomes a local representative with a specific constituency to answer to.

**Consensus voting for the Senate and Presidency.** The current system selects whoever gets the most first-choice votes, which means the most passionate minority wins even when a large majority prefers someone else. The alternative — used in single-seat races — runs head-to-head matchups across all candidates and selects the one who beats everyone in a direct comparison. Not the most intensely loved: the most broadly acceptable. This single change shifts the entire incentive structure of electoral politics. Being widely acceptable beats being worshipped by a faction. Building coalitions beats base mobilization. The simulation confirms it: in both senate configurations and the presidential race, the winner is the candidate who sits near the center of the full electorate, not the one who most activates any single bloc.

**Democracy Dollars.** Every registered voter receives $300 in vouchers — $100 each for House, Senate, and presidential races — to give to any candidate they choose. To accept them, candidates must cap private donations at $100 per person and reject PAC money. The private donor class, which currently funds the entire political system from a pool of less than 1% of the population, is outspent four-to-one — not by the government imposing anything, but by the voters themselves reclaiming the power of the purse. The $100 community voucher and the $100 private donation carry equal weight. The fundraising advantage currently held by candidates with wealthy networks evaporates. Candidates who want public funding have to come home and ask their neighbors for it.

## What This Congress Actually Does

The legislative simulation models 37 binary policy questions across both chambers. The striking finding is not any single result — it is the pattern.

**The tax paradox.** The same congress that extends the 2017 tax cuts also raises the corporate rate from 21% to 28% and allows top-bracket income tax rates on earnings over $400,000 to rise to 35%. These are not compromise positions or split-the-difference deals. They are three separate majority coalitions forming around three separate votes. Tax cut continuation backed by the center-right. Corporate rate increases backed by economic populists from the left and right — Solidarity, Reform, and the Social Democrats all support it. Upper-bracket rate increases backed by the left-leaning majority. In a two-party system these are on opposite party platforms. In a proportional congress they are three different bills with three different majorities.

**The immigration paradox.** The same congress that passes a permanent DACA pathway and grants legal status to long-term undocumented immigrants also increases border patrol funding and denies asylum to those crossing illegally. The DACA coalition runs through Social Democrats, Solidarity, Liberal, Center, and the centrist senate blends. The border enforcement coalition runs through Conservative, Reform, and Nationalist. They are different majorities — and both exist, which is why both bills pass.

**The gun paradox.** The assault rifle ban passes. Universal background checks pass with near-certainty. Concealed carry expansion fails. Gun regulation and gun rights both have majorities — just not the same majority. Background checks pass with broad support because 72% of Conservatives and 76% of Reform voters support them, despite what their party's official positions and their representatives' voting records suggest.

**What fails** are things that are actual minorities: ACA repeal (only extreme-right parties support it), restricting women from traveling for abortion (fails decisively — even Conservative senators won't restrict citizens' physical movement across state lines), post-9/11 surveillance program renewal (civil libertarians across the left provide the margin against it), halting all new oil and gas leases (the SD/Liberal/Progressive coalition is too small). These policies fail because they don't have 50% support in any realistic coalition. The current system amplifies them. The reformed system does not.

One item is a genuine toss-up: relaxing local zoning laws to allow more apartments, at exactly 50.2% national support. That one the country is actually split on.

## Why the Moment Is Now

Three crises are converging that make this more urgent than it has been in decades.

The Supreme Court's *Loper Bright* decision in 2024 dismantled the legal doctrine that let Congress pass vague laws and let agencies fill in the technical details. For 40 years this was how the American government functioned: Congress provided general authority, agencies wrote the actual rules. With that gone, and a revival of the non-delegation doctrine accelerating through the courts, Congress must be capable of writing specific technical rules itself — or the government cannot regulate anything effectively. A legislature that cannot pass a budget without a constitutional crisis is not a legislature equipped to write FDA drug approval standards or FAA drone flight path regulations. The electoral dysfunction isn't just politically frustrating. It is becoming a government functionality problem with a hard legal deadline.

Technological change is accelerating past the speed of the legislative process. AI systems, gene editing tools, and cryptocurrency infrastructure are evolving on monthly timescales. The congressional committee process works on an 18-month cycle. The result is a governance vacuum where unelected private-sector actors make consequential public decisions by default — not because they were granted authority but because no institution capable of exercising authority exists. The representative republic's ability to govern itself is running behind the pace of the things it needs to govern.

The legitimacy crisis is structural, not cyclical. The sense that elections don't represent real preferences, that both parties are captured by donors and extremists, that voting for your actual values is functionally irrational — these are not irrational perceptions. They are accurate descriptions of how the current system works. The 44% non-participation rate among Solidarity voters is not disengagement. It is feedback.

New Zealand transitioned from first-past-the-post to multi-party proportional representation in 1996. The first election was chaotic. Within two electoral cycles, coalition government was normalized, parties had differentiated themselves meaningfully, and voters had learned that voting sincerely was rational. It was rough. It was also clearly better.

## What the Simulation Proves

The nine parties in this simulation are not invented. They are the result of running a clustering algorithm on how actual Americans answer questions about their own lives. Solidarity exists because there are real people — majority female, majority non-white, lowest-income, evangelical, electorally skeptical, economically center-left — who have no current political home and therefore largely do not vote. Center exists because there are real people who voted for Trump and trust elections and support background checks and Medicaid expansion, and that combination makes no sense in a two-party frame but makes perfect sense as a distinct constituency with its own coherent view of the world.

The current system does not create these people. It just fails to represent them.

The design failure argument is ultimately an optimistic one. If the problem were cultural — if Americans were simply too polarized, too tribal, too angry to function in a democracy — the fix would require changing human nature. If the problem is design, the fix is a redesign. Three mechanisms that already exist, that work, that have been implemented in comparable countries, that require no change in who Americans are or what they believe.

The polarization is real. But it lives inside a system designed to amplify it — to force everyone into one of two camps and then run the camps against each other until someone wins 50.1%. Remove that constraint and what emerges is not a utopia. It is nine imperfect parties representing nine imperfect constituencies, building shifting coalitions on different questions, and passing legislation that reflects what the country actually wants rather than what the winning faction can impose.

The simulation is a proof of concept. Not of what American politics should look like. Of what American politics *already is* — waiting for a system capable of expressing it.
